My Heroes Journal

Handover Document · Power2ADAPT · For Claude Code
Purpose of this document: This is a comprehensive brief for building My Heroes Journal — a printed or digital PDF/HTML reflection and progress journal for student athletes and their parents. It documents the full content library from the My Athletic Journey (MAJ) progressive web app so the journal can stand alone as an alternative engagement tool.

1. Project Overview

What is My Heroes Journal?

My Heroes Journal is a companion document to Power2ADAPT's My Athletic Journey (MAJ) app. While the app tracks weekly progress digitally, the journal gives students a physical (or printable digital) record of their 32-week Level 1 journey. Parents can read it to understand what their child is learning each week; students can write in it as an alternative to the app's reflect tab.

Key audiences

🧒 Students (ages 8–16)
A personal journal they feel proud to own. Each weekly spread has the lesson concept, their session activity, and a reflection prompt with space to write. Achievements and badges are celebrated visually.
👨‍👩‍👧 Parents
A progress report they can actually read. Shows what their child is working on each week, what skills they are developing, and how they are growing as an athlete and person — not just scores and times.

Format options

  • HTML (primary) — A beautifully designed single-page or multi-page HTML file that can be saved as PDF from the browser, or hosted as a web page.
  • Print PDF — Designed for A4 printing. Possibly 2 versions: a full Level 1 journal (all 32 weeks), and a per-module booklet (8 weeks each).
  • Interactive HTML — Optionally, fields where students can type their reflections online.

Brand / Design Direction

  • Primary colour: #F26522 (Power2ADAPT orange)
  • Secondary colour: #00B4D8 (cyan, used for Module 3)
  • Typography: Barlow Condensed (headings, display) + DM Sans (body). Both free on Google Fonts.
  • Tone: Bold, motivating, sporty. Feels like a premium training journal, not a school workbook.
  • Print-friendly: White background, high contrast, avoid large dark fills when printing.
  • Space for writing: Each reflect section needs ruled lines or a generous box for handwritten responses.

3. Full Content Library — All 4 Modules

The following is the complete week-by-week content extracted from MAJ_CONTENT.modules in the app. Each week entry has learn, do, and reflect sections.

🔥 Module 1: Ignite Your Engine  ·  Pillar: Athletic Ability  ·  8 Weeks

This module builds the physical foundation — speed, power, coordination, endurance, and movement quality. Athletes discover their starting point, train across all physical qualities, and end with a coach-assessed capstone.

Week 1
Know Your Engine
Learn

Concept: What is Athletic Ability — and Why Does it Matter?

Athletic ability is not something you are born with — it is built. Key parts: Speed, Power, Coordination, Endurance, Movement Quality. Every elite athlete started exactly where you are now.

Do

Baseline Movement Assessment

Coach observes run, jump, throw, change of direction. Not a test — a starting point. Ask coach: What is one thing I do well, and one to improve? Keep notes to compare at Week 8.

Reflect

"What does being an athlete mean to me?"

Think about what kind of athlete you want to become — not just what you can do now, but who you want to be.

Week 2
Move Well
Learn

Concept: Why Movement Quality Comes Before Speed

Good movement is the foundation everything else is built on. Skipping, jumping, and running require coordinated patterns. "Slow is smooth. Smooth is fast. Quality first, speed second." — P2A coaching principle

Do

Agility Circuit + Out-and-Back Run

5-station agility circuit (ladder, weaving cones, hurdles, dodge and weave, skater hops), then steady out-and-back run matching pace each way. Pick ONE cue to apply across every station.

Reflect

"Which movement felt hardest today — and why do you think that is?"

Was it coordination? Did fatigue break your technique? Did a specific station catch you off guard?

Week 3
Speed
Learn

Concept: What Creates Speed — Fast Starts, Arms, and Reaction

Speed comes from: powerful push-off, fast arm drive, forward lean from ankles. Arms control legs. First 10m is where races are won. Cue: "Head still, short fast arms, fast feet."

Do

Fast Starts + Partner Chase

Practise fast starts from different positions (standing, crouching, 3-point). Then Partner Chase — one person starts 1m ahead, other must tag them before 10m mark.

Reflect

"When did you feel fastest today — and what were you doing differently in that moment?"

Arm drive? More relaxed? Different start position? One rep where everything clicked?

Week 4
Power ⚡ Power Up Badge (25 XP)
Learn

Concept: Jump, Bound, and Throw — How to Generate Explosive Power

Power = strength applied quickly. Two types trained: lower body (jumping/bounding) and upper body (throwing). Cues: "Jet wings on take-off. Reach for the sky. Motorbike landing."

Do

Bounding Hoops + Hop-Step-Jump

Bounding hoops — bound through a line of hoops for distance. Hop-step-jump — hop over hurdle, bound to dot, land in hoop. Drive arms hard from push-off. Soft landings = skilled, not weak.

Reflect

"Where in your body did you feel the most power today — and what helped you generate it?"

Arms, legs, core? Did arm drive change your jump height? Did knee lift change bounding distance?

Week 5
Hurdles & Relay
Learn

Concept: Hurdle Rhythm, Baton Changes, and Running as a Team

Hurdle = attack at speed with controlled step-over; lead leg clears, trail leg whips through. Relay won/lost in changeover zone. Cues: "Toes up, knees up, use arms. Arm straight back, call to run, go!"

Do

Hurdle Relay — Team Best Time

Race over two hurdle lines, then full relay with baton changes. Goal: improve team time by running better technique, not just harder. Incoming runner calls "hand"; outgoing extends arm straight back and accelerates immediately.

Reflect

"What was harder — the hurdles or the relay exchange — and what did you do to get better at it?"

Identify one specific moment you improved. What changed — a cue, a focus shift, or something a teammate said?

Week 6
Endurance
Learn

Concept: The Endurance Challenge — Running at Your Own Pace

Aerobic base built by sustained moderate effort — not flat out. If you can't hold a short conversation, you're too fast. Mental toughness: when hard, focus on the next 30 seconds, not the whole remaining time. Cue: "Relaxed shoulders, steady breathing, head still."

Do

Age Endurance Challenge + Curve Running

Run continuously for half your age in minutes without stopping (e.g. age 12 → 6 minutes). Every complete minute without walking = 10 points. Then curve running — practise hugging the inside of a 50m curve.

Reflect

"What was hardest about the endurance challenge — and what strategy did you use to keep going?"

Boredom, pain, or urge to walk? What did you tell yourself? Did early pacing actually help at the end?

Week 7
Putting it Together
Learn

Concept: Speed + Strength + Coordination + Endurance — All in One Session

Skipping = coordination + power. Plyometrics (pogos, squat jumps) = explosive power. Strength exercises = foundation. Circular relay = speed while tired. Throw accuracy = brain-body skill under fatigue. Notice how much more capable you are than Week 1.

Do

Fitness Circuit + Circular Relay + Throw for Accuracy

Four areas: skipping/plyometrics, strength exercises (push-ups, lunges, squats, planks), circular relay, overarm throw for accuracy. Set one personal target before starting. Coach it high-5 at changeover.

Reflect

"What has improved most since Week 1 — and how do you know?"

Identify a specific moment today where you were better than when you started. What was different — in your body, technique, or mindset?

Week 8
Capstone — My Athletic Highlight Reel 🏆 Module 1 Master Badge (50 XP) — Coach Required
Learn

Concept: Looking Back to See How Far You Have Come

Week 1 = starting point. Week 8 = measure of progress. Athletic development is not a straight line — some weeks were harder. Every session, every reflection, every challenge counts. After today, Module 2 — Skill — begins.

Do

Coach Assessment + Personal Best Attempt

Coach compares movement quality to Week 1 baseline. Athlete chooses personal best challenge: Sprint relay, Squat jump, or Tennis Ball Target. Coach awards Module 1 Master badge upon completion.

Reflect

"What am I most proud of from Module 1 — and what will I do differently in Module 2?"

Not about what you achieved. About who you became. What habits will you carry forward? What will you do better?

🎯 Module 2: Master Your Skills  ·  Pillar: Skill  ·  8 Weeks

Athletes choose one skill to develop for the entire 8 weeks. Focuses on deliberate practice, footwork, pressure performance, game application, and a Week 8 coach-assessed skill showcase.

Week 1
What is Skill?
Learn

Concept: Skill is Repeating a Movement Correctly Under Pressure

Skill is not talent — it is deliberate repetition with attention. Every athlete has skills they are strongest at and ones to develop. This module: choose one skill and build it across 8 weeks.

Do

Choose Your Skill — Set Your Goal

Talk with coach about skill options (throwing, catching, footwork, jumping, sport-specific). Write: "My skill focus for Module 2 is..." Agree on what success at Week 8 looks like. Complete 15 quality reps. Rate current level out of 10.

Reflect

"What skill do I most want to improve — and why does it matter to me?"

How does this skill connect to the sports or activities you love? Why is getting better at it important personally?

Week 2
Repetition 🔁 Rep Champion Badge (25 XP)
Learn

Concept: Why Repetition Builds Skill — The Brain-Body Connection

Brain learns movement like a trail through a forest — more use = clearer path. Quality over quantity: 20 focused reps beat 100 lazy ones. Early reps are slow; later reps become automatic. Mistakes are part of learning.

Do

20 Quality Repetitions Challenge

Not speed — quality. 20 reps with full focus on technique. Check in every 5 reps: "Am I still doing it correctly?" If technique breaks, slow down. Rate quality out of 10. Optional: ask coach to film last 5 reps and watch back.

Reflect

"What did you notice about your movement after 20 focused repetitions?"

Did it start to feel more automatic? Did you find something not quite right? What will you fix next week?

Week 3
Footwork
Learn

Concept: Footwork is the Foundation of All Athletic Skill

Where your feet are and how fast they move determines everything else. Poor footwork forces the upper body to compensate. Fast feet come from light ground contact. The best athletes in every sport have exceptional footwork — never an accident.

Do

Footwork Ladder or Cone Drill Challenge

Walk through pattern slowly first. 3 runs for foot placement (not speed). Coach records time on 4th full-speed run. 3 more runs to improve time while keeping clean footwork. Note fastest clean time to beat in future.

Reflect

"How does your footwork affect everything else you do as an athlete?"

Think about a moment when your feet were in a bad position. What happened to the rest of your movement as a result?

Week 4
Throw and Catch
Learn

Concept: How Accuracy and Timing Work Together

Throwing accuracy = consistent release point + follow-through. Catching starts with the eyes — track object all the way into hands. Communication between passer and receiver makes both better. Pressure reveals true skill level.

Do

20 Consecutive Catches — Partner Challenge

Work with a partner. 20 consecutive catches without a drop — drop = restart from zero. Start 3m apart. When you reach 20, both take one step back and try again. Record longest streak and furthest distance.

Reflect

"What did your partner do that helped you catch better — and what did you do to help them?"

Quality of throws, communication, encouragement. How did working together change your performance?

Week 5
Agility 💨 Agility Ace Badge (25 XP)
Learn

Concept: Agility — Changing Direction Without Losing Speed

True agility is reactive — responding to things happening around you. Change of direction requires good deceleration first. Low body position and bent knees = stable push-off base. Eyes lead body. Agility is trainable through cone drills and reaction games.

Do

Agility Course — Time Trial

Walk through course once. 70% speed run for clean direction changes. Coach records first full-speed time. 3 more attempts to improve. Record best time — will retest in Week 7. Optional: coach calls Left/Right at decision cone.

Reflect

"What slowed you down on the agility course — and what is your plan to fix it?"

Body position? Foot placement on turns? Acceleration out of the change? Be honest and specific.

Week 6
Skill Under Pressure
Learn

Concept: Why Skills Break Down When You Are Tired or Competing

Under pressure, brain reverts to older, less refined movement patterns. Physical fatigue makes technique harder. Mental pressure creates tension that disrupts smooth movement. Solution: practise skill in pressured conditions. Focus on process, not outcome.

Do

Skill Under Fatigue Challenge

Complete chosen skill from Week 2 — but after a full warm-up circuit that leaves you genuinely tired. 20 quality skill repetitions immediately after circuit. Notice how technique feels different when tired. Rate quality out of 10 and compare to Week 2.

Reflect

"Was your skill better or worse when you were tired — and what did you do to keep going?"

What strategy helped maintain technique when hard? What fell apart first? What held up best?

Week 7
Game Situation
Learn

Concept: Applying Your Skill in a Team Game Context

Game situations require reading what is happening and then executing. Decision-making speed is part of skill — right skill at wrong time = still an error. Communication with teammates makes game skills more effective. Mistakes in games are normal — what matters is your response.

Do

Game Activity — Skill in Action

Coach runs a game activity. Consciously apply Module 2 skill focus during the game. Look for moments to use your skill — don't force it but don't avoid it. After each round self-check: "Did I use my skill? Was it effective?" Ask a teammate what it looked like.

Reflect

"When in the game did you use your skill well — and what made that moment possible?"

Positioning, decision-making, timing. What set you up to execute successfully in a live situation?

Week 8
Skill Showcase — Capstone 🏆 Module 2 Master Badge (50 XP) — Coach Required
Learn

Concept: Showing What You Have Learned

8 weeks ago you chose a skill and rated yourself. Today you demonstrate what you have developed. A showcase is not about perfection — it is about honest progress and the habits that created it. After today, Module 3 — Focus — begins.

Do

Coach-Assessed Skill Demonstration

Warm up specifically for your skill. Complete 20 quality reps with coach observing. Rate skill out of 10 — compare to Week 1 rating. Ask coach: "What is the biggest technical improvement you have seen over 8 weeks?" Set one goal to continue into Module 3.

Reflect

"How has this skill changed for me over 8 weeks — and what made the difference?"

Specific habits, cues, and moments that created improvement. What will you keep doing? What did you learn about how you develop as an athlete?

🧠 Module 3: Train the Mind  ·  Pillar: Focus  ·  8 Weeks

Develops the mental performance toolkit: focus, breathing, self-talk, attention control, pressure management, and team focus. Athletes build skills they carry into sport, school, and life.

Week 1
What is Focus?
Learn

Concept: Focus is a Skill You Can Train — Not Something You Either Have or Do Not

Focus = directing attention to what matters most, right now. Most athletes have never been taught how to focus. Distractions are everywhere; focus is choosing what to pay attention to despite them. Attention is trainable.

Do

Mindful Warm-Up Challenge

Coach leads 2-minute quiet focus routine before session. During session, notice 3 moments when attention fully locks in and 3 moments when it drifts. After session, write what you noticed.

Reflect

"What makes it hard for me to focus during training — and when is my focus at its best?"

What conditions help you lock in? What pulls you away?

Week 2
Breathing and Calm
Learn

Concept: How Breathing Controls Your Body and Your Mind

Breathing is the only automatic function you can also control consciously. Shallow, fast breathing = danger signal to brain. Slow, deep breathing = safe, in control. The 4-7-8 technique: breathe in 4 counts, hold 7, breathe out 8. Breathing can't remove pressure — but changes how your body responds to it.

Do

4-7-8 Breathing Before Your Hardest Activity

Before hardest activity: stand still, relax shoulders. Breathe in through nose for 4 counts. Hold 7 counts. Breathe out slowly through mouth for 8 counts. Repeat twice, then go straight into the activity. Also use as reset tool after mistakes.

Reflect

"Did breathing help you feel more in control — and how did it change your performance?"

Compare normal day vs. after breathing exercise. Was there a difference? What was it?

Week 3
Self-Talk
Learn

Concept: The Words in Your Head Affect Your Performance

Negative self-talk creates tension and reduces performance. Positive self-talk is not pretending — it is choosing words that help. Cue words: short powerful phrases for key moments (Drive, Relax, Reset, Trust it). Elite athletes all use self-talk deliberately — it is trained, not accidental.

Do

Build Your Self-Talk Toolkit

Write 3 encouraging phrases for when training gets hard (personal, should feel true). Choose one cue word for today. Use cue word out loud (quietly) at least 5 times during session. After mistake — use self-talk instead of going silent or negative.

Reflect

"What did you say to yourself when things got tough today — and did it help?"

Were the words helpful or unhelpful? What is one phrase you will keep because it genuinely helped?

Week 4
Attention Control 🌬️ Calm Under Pressure Badge (25 XP)
Learn

Concept: The Difference Between Focusing on the Task and Focusing on the Outcome

Task focus = what you are doing right now. Outcome focus = what might happen as a result. Outcome focus during execution creates anxiety and disrupts automatic movement. Simple cue: "What do I need to do RIGHT NOW?"

Do

Task Focus Drill — Process Over Outcome

Choose one activity to be your task focus activity. Identify specific task focus: arm drive, soft landing, eyes on target. Every time mind goes to score/outcome, gently bring it back to task cue. Rate task focus out of 10.

Reflect

"What happened to your performance when you focused only on the task — not the outcome?"

More automatic? More controlled? Hard to stop thinking about the score? What does that tell you about where your attention usually goes?

Week 5
Pressure and Nerves
Learn

Concept: Why Nerves are Normal — and How to Use Them

Nerves = body getting ready to perform. Increased heart rate, alert senses, adrenaline are all performance tools. The feeling of nerves is almost identical to excitement — you can choose the label. Trying to make nerves disappear usually makes them worse. Repeated pressure exposure builds confidence.

Do

Mini Competition — Pre-Performance Routine

Design a 30-second pre-performance routine: breathing + cue word + one physical reset (roll shoulders, shake hands). Use it before every turn in the competition. Notice how you feel first turn vs. last turn. Share routine with coach.

Reflect

"How did you feel before the challenge — and what helped you settle and perform?"

Describe your nerves honestly. What did they feel like? What did you do? What made the biggest difference once it started?

Week 6
Consistency 📊 Consistent Performer Badge (25 XP)
Learn

Concept: Focus and Consistency are Directly Connected

Consistent performance comes from consistent focus, not talent. Easy to focus when fresh/excited — real skill is focusing when tired or struggling. Building a pre-session routine = mental consistency. Consistent focus compounds over time — small improvements each week = major development over a season.

Do

Full Session Focus and Effort Log

For every activity: rate effort/10 and focus/10. Set target before session (e.g. effort 8, focus 7). After each activity, rate quickly. At end, identify highest and lowest focus moments. Show log to coach and discuss one insight.

Reflect

"When was your focus highest this session — and what was different at those moments?"

Look at your log. What activity, moment, or condition produced your best focus? Can you recreate it deliberately?

Week 7
Focus in a Team
Learn

Concept: How Your Focus — or Lack of It — Affects the People Around You

Your energy and attention are contagious. A focused athlete raises the standard around them. Team focus built through shared purpose. The most focused teams communicate more, reset faster, and maintain higher intensity for longer.

Do

Team Relay — Pre-Activity Focus Agreement

Before relay: team spends 60 seconds agreeing on one shared focus cue (e.g. "drive hard off the mark"). Say it together before each race. If focus drops, one teammate calls the cue to re-engage. After relay: discuss as team when focus helped and when it was lost.

Reflect

"How did our team's collective focus change the result — and what was your role in maintaining it?"

When was team focus strong? When did it drop? What did you personally do to keep it or bring it back?

Week 8
My Focus Toolkit — Capstone 🏆 Module 3 Master Badge (50 XP) — Coach Required
Learn

Concept: Building a Mental Performance Toolkit You Will Use for Life

Your mental performance toolkit = the strategies that work best for you personally. Different athletes respond to different tools. A toolkit is only useful if you actually use it — the goal is to make these tools automatic. After today, Module 4 — Leadership — begins.

Do

Full Session Using Your Complete Toolkit

Use all three mental tools deliberately: breathing, cue word, task focus — especially under pressure and after mistakes. Before session: write down your three tools and when you plan to use each. After each activity: did I use my tools? At end: tell coach your tools and give one example of using each.

Reflect

"What are the 3 focus tools I will keep using beyond this module — and why do they work for me?"

Be specific about each tool. When do you use it? What does it feel like? What does it do for your performance? These are yours to keep forever.

🤝 Module 4: Lead Yourself and Others  ·  Pillar: Leadership  ·  8 Weeks

Leadership begins with leading yourself — your effort, attitude, and response to difficulty. Athletes build a leadership identity across 8 weeks: self-leadership, teammate skills, communication, handling mistakes, feedback, competition, personal leadership style, and a Level 1 Capstone.

Week 1
What is Leadership?
Learn

Concept: Leadership Starts With Leading Yourself — Before Leading Others

Self-leadership = taking responsibility for your own effort, attitude, and growth without being told. You do not need a title to be a leader — you lead through your actions. Leadership is not about being loud or best. It is about being consistent and trustworthy.

Do

My Self-Leadership Commitment

Three areas of self-leadership: effort, attitude, reliability. Choose one area to develop most over next 8 weeks. Write: "This module I will lead myself better by..." Share commitment with coach. Make one deliberate leadership action during session.

Reflect

"What kind of leader am I right now — and what kind of leader do I want to become?"

What are you already doing well as a self-leader? What is your biggest growth area? Where do you want to be at the end of Module 4?

Week 2
Being a Great Teammate 💬 Encourager Badge (30 XP) — Coach Required
Learn

Concept: The Difference Between Being Good and Being Great to Be Around

Great teammates encourage others — especially when things go wrong. Being a great teammate = supportive AND demanding high standards. How you respond to your own mistakes sets the tone for how the whole team responds. Noticing and celebrating others' good work is a leadership act.

Do

Encourage 3 Teammates — Intentional Support Challenge

Before session, identify 3 teammates to focus on encouraging. During session, find a specific moment where each does something well and name it (not just "Good job" — be specific: "Great arm drive on that sprint"). Deliver encouragement in the moment.

Reflect

"How did encouraging others change how you felt and performed in the session?"

Did focusing outward take your mind off your own performance? Did it increase your energy? How did the session feel different?

Week 3
Communication
Learn

Concept: How Athletes Communicate — Words, Body Language, and Energy

Communication in sport is more than words. Your body language, eye contact, energy, and positioning all send messages. Positive body language lifts everyone around you. Clear verbal communication: call for the ball, signal your intent. Listening is as important as speaking.

Do

Relay Communication Challenge

During relay, every handover must include a clear verbal and physical signal — no silent handovers. Before relay: agree as team on handover signal (a word + gesture). Practise it 5 times standing still. If handover is silent, that team runs the lap again. Standard is clear communication every time.

Reflect

"What did you do to communicate better with your team today — and what impact did it have?"

Think about both verbal and non-verbal. When did clear communication help most? When did poor communication cause a problem?

Week 4
Handling Mistakes
Learn

Concept: How Leaders Respond to Errors — Their Own and Others

Your response to a mistake is more important than the mistake itself. A reset cue is a physical or verbal action that signals to your brain: that is done, I am moving on. Accountability without self-punishment: acknowledge, identify what to do differently, move on.

Do

Reset Cue Practice

Coach models a reset cue. Build and practise your own: a physical action (clap, short breath out, tap thigh) + a word (Next, Reset, Done). Practise 10 times before session. Use it every time you make a mistake during session. Tell coach your cue and demonstrate it at end.

Reflect

"How did you handle a mistake or frustration today — and what would you do the same or differently next time?"

Did your reset cue help? Was there a moment you responded poorly? What triggered it, and what would a leader have done instead?

Week 5
Giving and Receiving Feedback
Learn

Concept: Feedback is a Leadership Tool — Giving it Well is a Skill

Effective feedback: specific, timely, focused on the action not the person. Feedback sandwich: one positive observation + one specific improvement + one encouraging close. Asking for feedback is strength, not weakness. Teams that give and receive feedback well improve faster.

Do

Feedback Partner Challenge

Pair with teammate. Agree on what skill/movement you will each observe. Watch partner during one activity — find something specific they are doing well. Deliver: "I noticed that you..., and it works because..." Receive partner's feedback with openness. Ask a follow-up question.

Reflect

"What was hard about giving feedback — and what was hard about receiving it?"

Which was harder for you, and why? What does that tell you about your leadership strengths and growth areas?

Week 6
Leading in Competition
Learn

Concept: How Pressure Reveals Leadership — Staying Steady When it Counts

Steady leadership under pressure = same effort, same communication, same reset response regardless of score. Panic is contagious. So is calm. The leader who stays composed gives everyone else permission to do the same. After a mistake in competition, your response in the next 10 seconds defines your leadership.

Do

Team Competition — Lift the Team Regardless of the Score

Before competition: agree as team on one leadership behaviour you will all bring. During: focus on leadership mission more than the score. When behind, find a way to lift energy — a word, reset cue, physical signal. After (win or lose): lead team reflection: "What did we do well? What do we build on?"

Reflect

"What did you do to help your team when things were hard — and how did they respond?"

Think about a specific moment of difficulty. What did you do? What impact did it have on team energy or performance?

Week 7
My Leadership Style
Learn

Concept: There is No One Type of Leader — Finding What Works for You

Common myth: leaders must be loud. In reality, some of the best sport leaders are quiet and consistent, leading entirely through their actions. Leadership styles: vocal/energetic · quiet/consistent · leading by example · leading through encouragement · leading through challenge. Your most effective style = the one that is authentic to who you are.

Do

Session Leadership Rotation

Coach nominates different session leader for each rotation. When it is your turn: lead the warm-up or activity setup, give clear instructions, encourage teammates, manage the activity. After rotation: ask 2 teammates "How did I go? What could I do better?" Observe others in their leadership turns.

Reflect

"What style of leadership felt most natural for me today — and what does that tell me about the leader I am becoming?"

When did you feel most comfortable and confident? Vocal or leading through action? What did teammates respond to best?

Week 8
The Athlete I Am Becoming — Level 1 Capstone 👑 Level 1 Complete — Legacy Athlete (100 XP) — Coach Required
Learn

Concept: Level 1 Is Complete — Reflecting on the Full Journey

32 weeks of training, reflection, and growth across Athletic Ability, Skill, Focus, and Leadership. The four pillars are not separate — they work together as one complete athlete. The habits you have built are your foundation for Level 2, Level 3, and everything beyond. Excellence Through Consistency is not a tagline. It is what you have lived.

Do

Level 1 Athletic Journey Reflection — Share With Your Coach

Complete Level 1 reflection (longer than normal — give it full effort). Sit with coach for 5 minutes. Share one thing most proud of from Level 1. Share one thing still want to develop in Level 2. Coach presents Level 1 completion and Journey Tier achievement — 👑 Legacy Athlete.

Reflect

"What has changed about me as an athlete and a person across this level — and what am I most proud of?"
